Overview
Four types, four meanings
Each conditional expresses a different relationship between condition and result.
1st Conditional
Real and possible situation in the present or future. The condition could actually happen.
2nd Conditional
Imaginary or unlikely situation now or in the future. The speaker doesn't expect it to happen.
3rd Conditional
Imaginary situation in the past. It's too late — it didn't happen. Used for regrets and speculation.
Mixed Conditional
A past condition with a present result — or a present condition with a past result.

The further right on the scale, the more "hypothetical" the situation — and the more complex the verb forms become.
1st Conditional
First Conditional — real possibilities
For real, likely situations in the present or future. The speaker believes this could actually happen.
Formula
If + present simple, … will + infinitive
If it rains, I will take an umbrella.
If you study hard, you will pass the exam.
She won't come to the party if she feels sick.
You can also use unless instead of if not: "Unless you hurry, you will miss the bus."
2nd Conditional
Second Conditional — imaginary present
For imaginary, unlikely or impossible situations now or in the future. The speaker doesn't expect this to happen.
Formula
If + past simple, … would + infinitive
If I had a million dollars, I would travel the world.
If I were you, I would apologise immediately.
She would move abroad if she spoke another language.
Use were (not was) for all persons in formal writing: "If I were rich…" This is correct and very common at B1+.
3rd Conditional
Third Conditional — past regrets
For imaginary situations in the past. It can't be changed now. Used for regrets, criticism, and speculation about the past.
Formula
If + past perfect, … would have + past participle
If I had studied, I would have passed.
If she had left earlier, she would have caught the train.
He wouldn't have got lost if he had brought a map.
Common mistake: don't use would have in the if clause. ✗ "If I would have known…" → ✓ "If I had known…"
Mixed Conditional
Mixed Conditional — past + present
When a past condition affects a present result — or a present condition relates to a past result.
Most common type (past → present)
If + past perfect, … would + infinitive
If I had studied medicine, I would be a doctor now.
If she had taken that job, she would be living in London now.
He would have finished university if he were more disciplined. (present → past)
The key is the time mismatch: one clause refers to the past, the other to the present. The tense in each clause signals which time it refers to.

Summary
The key question to ask
When you see a conditional, ask yourself: when is the situation, and how real is it?
1st — Real future
If + present → will
"This could really happen."
2nd — Imaginary now
If + past → would
"This probably won't happen."
3rd — Imaginary past
If + past perfect → would have
"This didn't happen — too late!"
Mixed — Time shift
Past condition → present result
"That past affected my present."
Practice tip: think of a real regret, a current dream, and a future plan — then write one sentence for each conditional type!
