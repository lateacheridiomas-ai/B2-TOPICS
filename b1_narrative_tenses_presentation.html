Narrative Tenses
The three tenses we use to tell stories about the past — what happened, what was happening, and what had already happened.
Past Simple
Past Continuous
Past Perfect
Overview
Three tenses, one story
Each tense has a different job when we narrate past events.
Past Simple
Completed actions — the main events of the story, in sequence.
Past Continuous
Background actions — what was already happening when something occurred.
Past Perfect
Earlier past — actions that happened before the main story began.

Think of Past Perfect as a "flashback" — it goes even further back than your story's starting point.
Past Simple
Past Simple — the main events
Form: subject + verb-ed (or irregular form). Used for completed actions in sequence.
She walked · He ate · They went
She woke up late, grabbed her bag and ran to the station.
The detective entered the room, looked around and found a note on the floor.
Signal words: yesterday, last night, in 2010, ago, then, finally, suddenly
Past Continuous
Past Continuous — the background
Form: was/were + verb-ing. Sets the scene or describes an action already in progress.
was raining · were talking · was sleeping
It was raining heavily when she arrived at the door.
They were having dinner when the phone rang.
The Past Continuous is the "stage setting" tense — it describes what was already happening. The Past Simple interrupts it.
Past Perfect
Past Perfect — the "before" tense
Form: had + past participle. Refers to something that happened before the main story moment.
had left · had never seen · had already eaten
When she arrived, he had already left.
She was nervous because she had never spoken in public before.
Use Past Perfect to show that one past action came before another past action — the extra "past".

Story
All three together
Read this short story and notice how the tenses work together.
It was raining and the streets were shining with light. Marco looked out of the window. He had promised to meet Ana at seven, but the bus had already left. He grabbed his coat, ran outside and called a taxi. Ten minutes later, he arrived at the café. Ana was still waiting for him, smiling.

Summary
Remember this
The three narrative tenses each answer a different question about the past.
Past Simple
"What happened?" → the main events, in order.
Past Continuous
"What was happening around it?" → background and scene-setting.
Past Perfect
"What had happened before?" → earlier actions, flashbacks.
Practice tip: try writing a 5-sentence story using all three tenses — it's the fastest way to make them feel natural!
